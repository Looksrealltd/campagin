The four phases
Phase 1 · Wks 1–2
Set up — brief, select, onboard
Creator pipeline confirmed, contracts signed, product shipped, briefs delivered. Every decision that will determine execution quality is made here. This phase is where most campaigns quietly fail — not in execution.
Phase 2 · Wks 3–4
Create — drafts, review, approve
Content comes in from Tier 1 and Tier 2 creators. Campaign Director reviews against brief. Client approves product claims only. 48-hour SLA governs every handoff. This is the highest-friction phase internally.
Phase 3 · Wks 5–6
Launch — publish, monitor, optimise
Content goes live in waves. Comment monitoring is active. Optimisation loop opens at day 5 post-publish. Best-performing content identified for paid amplification. Mid-campaign report delivered to client at end of week 6.
Phase 4 · Wks 7–8
Amplify and close
Paid whitelist amplification on top performers. Tier 3 micro-creator wave live. 30-day follow-up content briefed to Tier 1. End-of-campaign report prepared. Renewal conversation initiated.
The Campaign Director's job in one sentence
Own the system, not just the tasks. Every decision I delegate is still my accountability. Every check-in I run is an early-warning mechanism. Every report I send is either building client trust or eroding it. The campaign does not run itself — but it should feel like it does from the outside.
Week by week
Every task, every week

The full task breakdown across all 8 weeks. What is happening, who is doing it, and what must be true before the next phase begins.

Week 1
Creator selection finalised
CD: finalise creator longlist using platform data — engagement rate, comment quality, audience demographics, no audience overlap between Tier 1 creators. Coordinator: begin outreach to confirmed creators. AD: client briefed on creator rationale. Legal: contracts templated and ready. Product: confirm shipping quantities and packaging per creator tier.
Week 2
Onboarding and product seeding
Coordinator: contracts signed and filed, product packs dispatched with tracking. CD: deliver creator briefs (per-tier, not one-size-fits-all). Brief covers campaign context, 3 non-negotiable brand messages, 3 off-limits topics, suggested formats, creative freedom scope. Creator confirmation calls scheduled — Tier 1 only, 15 min each. CD: share campaign tracker with client. Approval workflow and SLAs communicated to client in writing.
Week 3
Tier 1 content drafts
Creators: submit content drafts via designated channel (not DM, not email). CD: review each draft against brief — product claims accuracy, off-limits topics, format fit. Not creative direction — do not rewrite their voice. Coordinator: chase any outstanding drafts (48hr after deadline, not day-of). CD: flag product claim issues to creator directly, not through client. Client review window opens for product claim approval only — 48hr SLA starts on submission.
Week 4
Tier 2 drafts + client approvals
Tier 2 creators: submit drafts. CD: review. Client: approves any Tier 1 product claims flagged. If client approval has not arrived within 48hrs, CD escalates to Account Director — not waiting. CD: confirm publish schedule with all approved creators, staggered across week 5. Coordinator: build publish tracker in shared workspace — date, platform, creator, status column. Standby creators confirmed if any Tier 1 withdrawals.
Week 5
Launch — Tier 1 and Tier 2 go live
Staggered publish: Tier 1 first (days 1–3), Tier 2 follows (days 3–5). CD: active comment monitoring for first 48hrs per post. Coordinator: log performance data into tracker within 24hrs of publish. CD: seed comment section engagement on Tier 1 posts via brand account if needed (subtle, not forced). Any negative comment requiring attention — CD responds protocol, not creator. Client: weekly status update sent end of week 5.
Week 6
Optimisation loop + mid-campaign report
CD: run optimisation review — day 5+ data for each published post. Decision tree: posts at 5%+ ER flagged for paid whitelist. Posts underperforming reviewed — was it brief misalignment, posting time, format? Document the read. Coordinator: pull platform data into report template. CD: write strategic insight layer — not just metrics, what they mean and what we do next. Mid-campaign report delivered to client. CD leads call to present findings and recommendation.
Week 7
Paid amplification + Tier 3 live
Performance team (or CD-led if in-house): whitelist amplification activated on approved Tier 1 content. Budget allocation based on week 6 decision tree. Tier 3 micro-creators: content goes live — lower oversight needed, UGC-style, monitored for volume not perfection. CD: brief Tier 1 creators on 30-day follow-up content (2nd piece). Coordinator: track paid performance separately in dashboard. Client: brief update on paid performance mid-week.
Week 8
Close, report, renew
CD: compile final campaign report — full performance data, comment sentiment analysis, creator ranking, strategic insights, recommended next steps. Coordinator: data pull and formatting. CD: present report to client — not email-only, live call. Renewal conversation initiated in this call, not after. CD: internal debrief with team — what worked, what we'd do differently, what we standardise. Creator relationships reviewed — who do we want to maintain for future campaigns?

Delegation principle
I delegate execution, not judgement. The Coordinator should never be making decisions about what is right for the campaign — only about whether things are done. Every strategic decision — creator selection, brief tone, optimisation call, report framing, renewal angle — sits with me. That is what the Campaign Director role is. Giving those away is not efficient delegation. It is abdication.
Check-in structure
The cadence that keeps everything visible

Check-ins are not status meetings. They are early-warning systems. The goal of every check-in is to find the thing that is about to go wrong — before it does.

Daily creator tracker review
Every morning · 10 min · CD solo
Who
Campaign Director, alone
Format
Open campaign tracker. Scan all creator statuses. Flag anything that has not moved since yesterday.
Outputs
Mental map of where pressure is building. Any chase messages triggered before 10am.
Why it matters
Most delays are visible 48hrs before they become problems. This habit is what prevents last-minute scrambles.
Internal team sync
Twice weekly · 20 min · CD + Coordinator (+ Creative if needed)
Who
CD + Coordinator. Creative joins in weeks 3–4 during content review.
Agenda
1. What is live or published this week. 2. What is stuck or delayed. 3. Any creator issues. 4. What does the client need to know before Friday.
Format
Standing call, 20 minutes hard stop. Tracker open on screen. No narrative — just status and decisions.
Outputs
Coordinator has a clear task list for the next 48hrs. CD has a clear view of any escalations needed before client update.
Weekly client update
Every Friday · Written + optional call · CD to client
Format
Short written update (not a full report). Three sections: what went live this week, what performed, what is happening next week.
Tone
Direct, confident, no hedging. If something is delayed, say so and say what is being done. Do not wait for the client to notice.
Calls
Offer a 15-min call if there is a decision pending or something complex to explain. Do not call for a status that can be written.
Why it matters
Clients who feel informed do not chase. Clients who chase are clients at risk of churning. This update is retention work, not admin.
Post-publish 48hr comment review
Within 48hrs of every Tier 1 + 2 post going live
Who
CD for Tier 1 and 2. Coordinator flags Tier 3 issues only.
What I look for
Sentiment pattern. Product claim disputes. Any negative narrative forming. Ingredient questions (these feed the next brief). Price objections (flag to client).
Action trigger
Any comment that requires a brand response — CD writes it, brand account posts it. Creator does not handle brand issues in their own comments.
Data use
Comment themes logged and fed into optimisation loop and mid-campaign report.
Optimisation loop review
Day 5 post-publish · CD-led · 30 min
Who
CD + Coordinator (data). Creative joins if brief changes are recommended.
Decision points
Which posts go to paid whitelist. Which creators get a re-brief before their next content. Which content pillars are outperforming — scale them in next wave.
Output
Written optimisation memo (1 page). Feeds directly into mid-campaign report to client.
Mid-campaign client report call
End of week 6 · 45–60 min · CD + client
Format
Structured presentation. Not a data readout — a strategic conversation. What is working, why, and what we are doing about the second half.
Key agenda
1. Campaign performance vs KPIs. 2. Creator ranking — who is delivering. 3. Comment section insights. 4. Paid amplification plan. 5. What we recommend for the final two weeks.
Renewal signal
This call is where renewal intent first surfaces. If the client is leaning in, the conversation about Q3 strategy begins here — not in a separate meeting later.
Risk register
What will go wrong — and the response

Every risk below is real, recurring, and manageable — if you have already thought about it before it happens. Click each one to see the full response protocol.

High severity
Tier 1 creator drops out mid-campaign
Illness, personal emergency, or brand conflict after signing
High ↓
Why it is serious
Tier 1 creators carry campaign credibility. A gap here is not just a content hole — it is a gap in the comment-section depth that the whole optimisation strategy depends on.
Prevention (before it happens)
Two standby creators per tier are identified and warm before launch. They have received the brief, confirmed interest, and are waiting. This is not optional — it is a campaign infrastructure decision made in week 1.
Response (if it happens)
1. Activate standby creator immediately — no client escalation needed yet. 2. If standby is not suitable, CD assesses whether Tier 2 creator can be elevated with a supplementary brief. 3. Client is notified only once a solution is confirmed — not during the problem. 4. No timeline slippage communicated unless unavoidable.
What not to do
Do not tell the client before you have a solution. Do not replace a Tier 1 creator with a reach-equivalent who does not meet the selection criteria. Speed is not more important than fit.
Creator publishes off-brief content without pre-approval
Wrong product claim, off-limits topic, or brand-unsafe language
High ↓
Why it is serious
A false product claim creates regulatory and brand risk. An off-limits topic (e.g. competitor mention, medical claim without disclaimer) can require content removal and damage client trust in the agency.
Prevention
The brief is designed to make compliance easy, not hard. Three non-negotiables stated clearly. Approval process for product claims is contractual. Creator confirmation calls for Tier 1 specifically cover this — not to police, but to make sure they genuinely understand the lines.
Response
1. CD contacts creator directly and privately within 2 hours of discovery. No public comment. 2. If content needs amendment — creator edits caption. If content needs removal — creator takes it down, CD provides revised language. 3. Client is informed proactively with resolution already underway. 4. If creator is uncooperative, contract clause is invoked. AD involved.
Client communication
"We identified a product claim in [creator]'s post that did not meet the brief standard. We have already resolved it — the updated version is live. Here is the new post." That sentence builds more trust than pretending it did not happen.
Client approval bottleneck — SLA breach
Client does not respond within 48 hours, blocking publish schedule
High ↓
Why it is serious
A delayed approval does not just affect one creator. It cascades — staggered publish schedule breaks down, creator availability shifts, paid amplification window shrinks.
Prevention
The 48-hour SLA is written into the contract and re-communicated at campaign kick-off. The approval window and what happens if it lapses is agreed in writing before the campaign starts — not during it.
Response
1. At 36 hours: CD sends a brief, direct nudge — "Just checking approval is on track for the [creator name] content — publish is scheduled for [date]." Not a chase, a heads-up. 2. At 48 hours with no response: CD escalates to Account Director. AD contacts client senior stakeholder. 3. If approval does not arrive by 60 hours and client cannot be reached: CD makes the call on whether content is safe to publish based on brief compliance — and documents that decision with rationale.
Longer-term fix
If a client consistently misses SLAs, this is flagged in the renewal conversation as a workflow risk — and the contract for the next campaign is structured with a different approval mechanism.
Medium severity
Campaign underperforms vs. KPI benchmarks
Engagement rate or sentiment significantly below target at week 6
Medium ↓
Response
Do not wait for the end-of-campaign report to name this. The mid-campaign report is the moment to address it directly with the client — with a clear diagnosis and a clear plan. "Engagement on lifestyle content is tracking lower than ingredient-led content. We are re-prioritising the second half brief accordingly." Clients respect honesty paired with action. They do not respect a final report that surprises them.
Diagnostic questions
Is underperformance consistent across creators (brief issue) or isolated (creator fit issue)? Is a specific content pillar underperforming? Is the platform split wrong? Answering these before the client call is the job.
Negative comment cluster on a creator post
Coordinated criticism, product dispute, or off-topic controversy
Medium ↓
Response
1. CD assesses whether comments are isolated or forming a pattern (within first 2 hours). 2. If product dispute: brand account posts a factual, calm response. Creator does not engage with brand disputes in their own comments. 3. If personal attack on creator: CD contacts creator privately, discusses whether they want support or prefer to manage it themselves. 4. If organised pile-on with reputational risk: AD and client notified. Decision made together on whether to leave, respond, or remove content.
What not to do
Do not delete legitimate critical comments about the product — it amplifies the perception of something being hidden. Respond factually instead.
Product not received by creator before content deadline
Shipping delay, wrong address, customs hold (especially cross-border)
Medium ↓
Prevention
All product ships at end of week 1 with tracking. Coordinator confirms receipt with every creator by end of week 2. No creator starts producing content without confirmed product receipt.
Response
1. If delayed: expedited shipping immediately, creator deadline extended by 5 days. 2. If extended delay (cross-border): CD contacts creator to arrange alternative — local purchase reimbursed, or product reshipped from regional stock. 3. Publish schedule adjusted. Client notified if it affects the week 5 launch.
Lower severity — still worth naming
Platform algorithm change mid-campaign
TikTok or Instagram changes distribution logic, suppressing organic reach
Lower ↓
Response
Reframe the impact before the client notices it. Reach is not the primary KPI on this campaign — engagement rate and comment depth are. An algorithm change that suppresses reach does not necessarily suppress engagement on content that is already resonating. Adjust paid amplification budget upward if organic reach drops significantly. Document the external factor in the mid-campaign report — clients respect context.
Creator produces technically compliant but creatively weak content
Nothing wrong in the brief sense — but it feels flat and forced
Lower ↓
Response
This is the hardest one to handle because it is subjective. The brief gave creative freedom — the creator used it, and it did not land. The response is a gentle coaching conversation, not a rejection. "This looks great — one small thought from my side. The moments where your content really resonates are usually when you share [specific thing they do well]. Would you be open to one more version that leans into that?" Give them the direction without taking their voice away.
Prevention
The creator confirmation call in week 2 is partly for this — to understand how the creator is planning to approach the content before they have already produced it.
Client stakeholder change mid-campaign
New marketing lead takes over the account partway through
Lower ↓
Response
Request a 30-minute onboarding call with the new stakeholder within 48hrs of the change. Send them the campaign overview and tracker before the call so they arrive informed. The goal is to make them feel that they have walked into a campaign that is running well — not one that needs their intervention. Do not wait to be introduced. Proactively reach out.
Escalation map
When to escalate — and to whom

Most Campaign Directors escalate too late or too often. The goal is to resolve at the lowest level possible — and escalate the moment you cannot. Never sit on a problem that requires senior involvement.

Decision tree
1
Can I resolve this myself within the next 24 hours without affecting the client or the campaign timeline? If yes — resolve it. Do not loop anyone in. Document what you did and why in the campaign log.
2
Does the issue require a decision that will affect the client relationship? If yes — notify the Account Director before contacting the client. Never surprise the AD with a client conversation they did not know was happening.
3
Does the issue involve a legal, regulatory, or commercial risk? Creator published false health claim. Brand is asking for something outside the contract scope. Legal or commercial team involved immediately. Do not try to resolve this at Campaign Director level.
4
Is the client expressing serious dissatisfaction? A single concern is a CD conversation. A pattern of dissatisfaction, or a threat to not renew, is an AD + CD joint response. The AD relationship with the client exists precisely for this.
5
Has something gone wrong that you have already resolved? Tell the client and the AD anyway. Proactive disclosure of a resolved problem builds significantly more trust than them discovering it later. "We had an issue with X — here is what happened and here is how we fixed it" is a trust-building sentence, not a confession.
Escalation routing by situation
Creator issue
CD handles directly
All creator-facing communication goes through the Campaign Director. The creator relationship is CD territory. AD is informed but does not intervene unless the issue is commercial (contract dispute, payment, legal).
Client communication
CD leads, AD informed
All regular client communication is CD-owned. The AD is looped in on all emails above the level of a status update — any strategic recommendation, any issue disclosure, any commercial conversation.
Performance shortfall
CD owns the narrative
Performance issues are owned and communicated by the CD — with a plan. The AD supports commercially if the client is at churn risk. Never ask the AD to deliver bad news that the CD should be delivering.
Brand safety incident
Immediate AD + client loop
Any content that creates brand, legal, or reputational risk is an immediate escalation to AD. CD and AD respond to client together. Response drafted before the call — do not go into that call without a written position.
The rule I operate by
If I am hesitating about whether to escalate — I escalate. The cost of involving someone unnecessarily is a brief conversation. The cost of not involving them when I should have is a client relationship. There is no version of this job where protecting my own sense of control is worth more than protecting the client's confidence in the team.
